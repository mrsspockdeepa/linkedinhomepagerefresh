UI / UX Case Study · 2026

Reclaiming the LinkedIn home page for the people who use it every day.

LinkedIn's web home page has drifted into a cluttered, low-density layout that wastes roughly 40% of its horizontal real estate on empty gutters while burying the content users actually come for. Broadsheet is a redesign proposal that treats the feed like an editorial product — dense, legible, and respectful of both the hiring managers scanning for talent and the candidates tracking their professional world.

01 The Problem

A home page that feels like a hallway, not a room.

The current LinkedIn web home page has inherited layers of layout decisions from a decade of product growth. The result is a three-column grid dominated by margins, a feed that shows one and a half posts above the fold, a profile card that consumes an entire column for information the user already knows, and a news rail with oversized typography that looks like an aggregator rather than a quick scan.

Users — both the hiring managers sourcing candidates and the professionals tracking their industry — consistently describe it as clunky, wasteful, and slow to scan. The problem isn't content; it's layout.

Issue 01

Wasted horizontal space

On a 1440px display, roughly 40% of the viewport is given to empty gutters and oversized padding. The feed column itself is narrower than the right rail deserves.
Issue 02

Truncated primary data

The profile card clips the user's own name ("Jen De…") and role ("Product Manager - 0 to 1 - …") on standard laptop widths — a jarring failure for a professional identity product.
Issue 03

Only one post above the fold

At a standard viewport, users see one full post and the top of a second. Scrolling is required before the feed feels like a feed at all.
Issue 04

News rail out of proportion

LinkedIn News uses text sized for a standalone article list, not a companion rail. Five headlines occupy more vertical space than four feed posts.
Issue 05

No density affordance

There's no way to see more at once. Power users — recruiters screening hundreds of profiles — have no density toggle, no keyboard navigation, no scan mode.
Issue 06

Weak visual hierarchy

Every element competes with the same visual weight. The eye has no entry point, so scanning defaults to top-to-bottom, left-to-right — the slowest possible reading pattern.
02 Goals

Four outcomes the redesign had to deliver.

Before touching a pixel, we defined what success looked like. Each goal had to be observable in the final design — not aspirational marketing language.

Reduce user frustration

Eliminate the truncation, wasted space, and low-density patterns that make the current page feel cluttered and unresponsive.

Make it aesthetically pleasing

Commit to an editorial point of view — serif headlines, confident typography, restrained color — so the product feels considered rather than accidental.

Serve hiring managers & candidates

Surface the fastest paths to value for each audience — matched roles, engagement signals, and direct action — without burying them behind navigation.

Optimize real estate

Use the full width of the viewport. Every column earns its place by showing content users actually want — not chrome.

04 Key Decisions

Six design moves that did most of the work.

Every design decision was made in service of one of the four goals above. Here are the six changes that delivered the biggest measurable improvement in density, legibility, and speed.

01

Replace the profile card with an icon rail

The left column gets a 56px vertical icon rail for primary navigation. Profile information moves to the right rail where it earns its keep with actual engagement stats.

Before
240px profile card with truncated name
After
56px icon rail — 184px reclaimed for feed
02

Masthead with a command-K search

The oversized search field collapses into a full-width ⌘K command bar with top-level category pins (Feed, Network, Jobs, Learning) visible at all times.

Before
Pill search + icon cluster
After
Command bar + persistent nav chips
03

Editorial feed with serif headlines

Posts render as proper editorial cards with a clear Roboto headline, muted body text, category chip, and right-aligned engagement counts. The eye has a clear entry point on every row.

Before
Uniform sans text, ~1.5 posts above fold
After
Serif headlines, 4–5 posts above fold
04

Condense LinkedIn News into a numbered index

News headlines become a ranked 01–05 list with smaller type, muted reader counts, and a hover state that previews the article. The whole rail takes a third of its former height.

05

Surface matched jobs for every user

A "Matched Roles · 94% fit" block sits in the right rail on every visit. Candidates always see the top three roles that match their profile. Hiring managers get a parallel block showing profiles that match their open reqs.

05 Who It Serves

Two audiences, one home page.

LinkedIn's home page has to do two jobs well. Hiring managers need signal. Candidates need visibility. Broadsheet gives each audience a dedicated surface without fragmenting the product.

Persona A

Hiring Managers & Recruiters

Scan-oriented users who open LinkedIn 8–15 times per day looking for signal — new candidates, updated profiles, responses to outreach. They need density and keyboard-level speed.

  • Four to five posts visible above the fold for faster signal scanning
  • Command bar with ⌘K for instant profile, company, and post search
  • Category filters (Top, Latest, Network, Industry) at the top of the feed
  • Engagement counts right-aligned for at-a-glance impact assessment
  • Matched candidate block always visible in the right rail
Persona B

Active & Passive Candidates

Professionals tracking their industry, sharing work, and watching for opportunities. They need visibility into their own reach and frictionless access to matched roles.

  • Profile engagement stats (157 viewers, 2.4K impressions) surfaced in the rail
  • Matched roles panel with fit percentage always visible
  • Compose bar with one-click Post / Article / Poll / Video
  • Category chips on every post for industry-filtered browsing
  • News as a scannable index, not a competing column
